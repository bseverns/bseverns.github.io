    <h1>Documentation as Interface</h1>
    <p>Documentation is not only what remains after the work. In this practice, it is one of the interfaces people use to enter, operate, repair, refuse, or inherit a system.</p>

    <h2>What documentation carries here</h2>
    <ul class="atlas-todo">
      <li>Operating steps, failure recovery, and maintenance habits.</li>
      <li>Consent language, retention boundaries, and refusal paths.</li>
      <li>Teaching transfer: how a tool, scene, or lesson survives beyond one operator.</li>
      <li>Source trails showing where the live implementation truth actually lives.</li>
    </ul>

    <h2>Projects that surface the method</h2>
    <ul class="atlas-todo">
      <li><a href="/atlas/n/machinedocs/">machine-docs</a> turns machine care into a public runbook and teaching object.</li>
      <li><a href="/atlas/n/liverig/">live-rig</a> treats setup and recovery as part of the scene system, not backstage residue.</li>
      <li><a href="/atlas/n/syllabusrepo/">Syllabus</a> keeps prompts, policies, and pathway structure portable across courses and workshops.</li>
      <li><a href="/atlas/n/humanbuffer/">Human-Buffer</a> makes consent and privacy legible through workshop materials and interface logic.</li>
    </ul>

    <h2>Reader path</h2>
    <p>When an Atlas page says a repo is the source of truth, that is part of the interface too. The public site carries claims and proof objects. The repo carries the live build details.</p>
